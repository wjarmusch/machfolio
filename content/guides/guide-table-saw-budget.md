Table saw prices run from a couple hundred dollars to several thousand, and the gap between them is real, not just marketing. The trick is knowing where the meaningful jumps are so you spend enough to get what you need without paying for capability you'll never use. Here's what your money buys as you move up.

## Under $300: portable and basic

At this level you're in jobsite saw territory. These are light, portable saws with aluminum tables and loud, high-revving motors built to be carried and run off a standard outlet. They cut, and for a beginner or someone doing a mix of construction and light woodworking, they do the job.

What you're accepting is a smaller table, more vibration, and less of the flat, dead-on accuracy that finer work rewards. If space is tight or you're just starting out, this is a reasonable place to begin. Just know that a good jobsite saw is the ceiling here, not a stepping stone you'll upgrade in place.

## $300 to $650: better jobsite and benchtop saws

Spending a bit more in this range gets you a more capable jobsite saw, often with a better fence, more power, and a sturdier build. The fence quality jump matters more than most buyers expect, because a saw is only as accurate as its fence, and cheaper fences are where budget saws cut corners.

This is a solid spend for a hobbyist who wants portability but takes accuracy seriously. You're still in portable-saw territory, but the good ones here punch above their size.

## $650 to $1,200: contractor and hybrid saws

This is where things get interesting for the serious hobbyist. You start reaching contractor and hybrid saws, with cast iron tables, induction motors that run quieter and smoother, and the enclosed cabinet bases that actually capture dust. The rigidity and cut quality take a real step up.

> For someone with a dedicated shop who's buying a saw to keep, the hybrid saws in this range are often the smartest spend on the whole ladder. You get most of what a cabinet saw delivers, on standard household power, without the price or the wiring a full cabinet saw usually needs. It's the level where a table saw stops feeling like a compromise.

The jump from a jobsite saw to a hybrid is the one most buyers feel the most, in accuracy, dust collection, and the simple pleasure of a saw that stays put and stays aligned.

## $1,200 to $2,500: entry cabinet saws and premium hybrids

Above the hybrid sweet spot you're paying for heavier construction, more power, and better fences and trunnions. Some saws here are premium hybrids, others are entry-level cabinet saws. Many start needing a 220-volt circuit, so factor your shop's power into the decision.

For a hobbyist whose work is serious and regular, or who simply wants a saw that will never be the limiting factor, this range delivers. For casual use, it's more saw than the work calls for.

## $2,500 and up: full cabinet saws

At the top are the full cabinet saws: heavy, fully enclosed, powerful induction motors, and the mass that soaks up vibration and holds alignment for the flattest, most accurate cuts you can get. These are built for daily use, production work, and buyers who put accuracy above everything.

They cost more, claim permanent floor space, and usually need dedicated power. But for the buyer who wants a saw that outlasts them and never holds their work back, a cabinet saw is the endgame.

## So how much should you spend

Start with your space and your power, because those rule options in or out fast. If you need portability or you're just starting, a good jobsite saw in the $300 to $650 range is a sensible spend. If you have a dedicated shop on standard power, the hybrid saws around $650 to $1,200 are where most serious hobbyists find the best balance, and it's the jump most worth stretching for. Full cabinet saws are worth it if you have the power, the space, and genuinely demanding work, and overkill if you don't.

Every machine on MachFolio carries a tier badge, from Weekend Warrior up to Professional. The tier is meant to be a quick way to understand where a machine fits before you dig into the specs. It aims to answer the question "who is this built for." Here's what each tier means and how we decide where a machine goes.

## Why tiers instead of just price

Price is the obvious way to sort machines, and it's part of how we tier them, but price alone can be misleading. A machine can be expensive because it's genuinely built for hard use, or expensive because of a brand name, or cheap because a company cut the exact corners that matter. 

So tiers combine three things: the price range, the build quality and materials, and how owners actually use the machine day to day based on what they report in the community. When those three point in different directions, real-world use and owner experience break the tie, not the sticker price.

## The five tiers

### Weekend Warrior

Entry-level machines built for light, occasional use. These are usually sold through mass-market retailers, often with lighter construction and smaller motors. There isn't much of an upgrade path, so when you outgrow one, you generally replace it rather than improve it.

This tier is right for someone testing whether they enjoy the hobby, doing occasional craft and project work, or buying their first machine on a tight budget. If the community consistently describes something as a good starter or a stepping stone, it lands here.

### Hobbyist

A step up in build and capability. You start seeing cast iron on the surfaces that matter, better motors, and enough quality that real work gets done without much fighting the tool. There's usually some upgrade path too, like better blades, fences, or mobile bases.

This is the buyer who's committed to woodworking as a hobby but hasn't yet prioritized shop-grade equipment. Mass-market house brands generally cap out at this tier no matter their price, because the community doesn't trust them at the level above.

### Serious Hobbyist

This is the heart of the range for most dedicated woodworkers. These machines assume you have a shop, you use your tools regularly, and you did your research before buying. Cast iron is standard, the fence and accessory ecosystem is real, and owners talk about upgrading and tuning these machines rather than replacing them.

If you're buying something you plan to keep for years and grow into, this is usually where you're looking.

### Semi-Pro

Built for near-daily use, production work, or buyers who put accuracy above everything else. You'll find heavier cast iron, precision-ground surfaces, and more motor. The people buying here are often serious hobbyists who outgrew the tier below, small shops, or teachers. Community conversation shifts from "is this good enough" to fine-tuning and getting the most out of an already capable machine.

### Professional

The top of the range. Industrial construction, continuous-duty motors, and often the need for dedicated power like a 220-volt circuit. These are for professional woodworkers, production shops, and institutions. The buyer pool is smaller and more specialized, so community discussion is thinner here, but these machines are built to run hard for decades.

## How a machine gets placed

We start with price to get in the right neighborhood, then adjust based on build quality and what owners actually report. A machine priced near a tier boundary gets placed by how the community treats it. If a saw costs a little less than the tier above but experienced owners consistently treat it as a serious tool, it moves up. If it costs a little more but owners describe it as a good beginner machine, it moves down.

> A couple of standing rules worth knowing. Mass-market house brands are capped at Hobbyist regardless of price, because community perception doesn't support placing them higher. And vintage or discontinued machines are tiered by their original standing and reputation, not by whatever they happen to sell for used today.

The goal is that the tier tells you something honest and useful at a glance, so you can spot the machines built for someone like you before you compare a single spec.

Table saw spec sheets lead with horsepower and a few big numbers, but the specs that most affect your day-to-day cutting aren't always the ones getting top billing. Some of what matters most doesn't even appear on the sheet. Here's how to read a table saw spec sheet and know what's actually worth your attention.

## Fence quality matters more than horsepower

If you take one thing from this guide, make it this. A table saw is only as accurate as its fence, because the fence is what sets the width of every cut. A saw can have a powerful motor and a flat table, and if the fence doesn't lock down square and stay put, you'll fight it on every cut.

The frustrating part is that fence quality is where budget saws cut corners, and it's not always obvious from the spec sheet. A "T-square" style fence that locks solidly is worth a lot. A flimsy fence that shifts is a daily annoyance no amount of motor power makes up for. This is the single spec-adjacent factor most worth caring about, and it's why owner feedback matters so much when comparing saws.

## Rip capacity: match it to your work

Rip capacity is how wide a cut you can make to the right of the blade, and it's genuinely useful to weigh, because it sets whether you can break down full sheets of plywood or handle wide panels. Bigger isn't automatically better though. A large rip capacity means a larger footprint, and if you don't cut wide material often, you're giving up shop space for reach you won't use. Match it to the work you actually do.

## Motor power, in context

Horsepower gets top billing on table saw marketing, and it does matter, especially for cutting thick hardwood without bogging down. But context matters more than the raw number. How the motor is rated, and whether it's a universal motor or an induction motor, affects real-world performance and longevity as much as the horsepower figure.

There's also a practical wrinkle: more powerful saws often need a 220-volt circuit, which your shop may or may not have. So motor power isn't just about capability, it's about whether your space can even run the saw. Factor your available power into the decision before chasing the highest number.

## Cut depth and blade tilt: check, don't obsess

Maximum depth of cut at 90 and 45 degrees tells you how thick a piece you can cut through, and it's worth a glance to confirm it covers your needs, though most saws in a given class are similar here. Blade tilt direction, left or right, is a preference that affects certain cuts and safety habits. Neither is usually a deciding factor, but they're quick to check.

## The specs that don't show up on the sheet

Like band saws, some of what most shapes a table saw's daily feel isn't a listed number.

Dust collection is a big one and it ties to the saw's design. Open-base contractor saws tend to collect dust poorly, while enclosed cabinet and hybrid bases capture it far better, and the spec sheet won't spell out how much of a difference that makes in practice. Build quality and how well the trunnions and table are machined affect alignment and how easily the saw stays tuned. The quality of the miter gauge, the smoothness of the adjustments, and how solid the whole saw feels under a cut all matter and none appear as specs.

> This is why comparing saws on specs alone leaves out half the picture. Two saws with similar numbers can feel completely different because the difference lives in the fence, the dust collection, and the build quality. Read what owners consistently say, not just the spec sheet, before you decide.

## How to read a table saw spec sheet, in short

Start with the fence, since it affects accuracy more than anything else, and lean on owner feedback because the spec sheet won't fully tell you. Match rip capacity to the width of material you actually cut. Weigh motor power against both your cutting needs and your shop's available power. Glance at cut depth and tilt to confirm they cover your work. Then read what owners report about dust collection and build quality, because those unlisted qualities often decide how much you'll enjoy the saw.

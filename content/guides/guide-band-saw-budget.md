The honest answer to "how much should I spend on a band saw" is that it depends entirely on what you plan to cut and how often. A saw that's perfect for someone cutting small curves would frustrate someone trying to resaw hardwood, and vice versa. Rather than throw a single number at you, here's what your money actually buys as you move up, so you can find the level that fits your work.

## Under $200: the entry point

At this level you're looking at benchtop saws, usually 9 or 10 inches, built for light and occasional use. They handle small curves, scrollwork, and craft projects fine. The motors are small, the resaw height is limited, and the construction is lighter, so they'll bog down in thick hardwood and won't hold up to heavy use.

This is the right spend if you're testing whether you enjoy the tool, or you know you'll only reach for it occasionally. What you're accepting at this price is limited capacity and a machine you'll likely replace rather than upgrade if the hobby takes hold.

## $200 to $500: real capability starts

Spending into this range gets you a meaningful step up. You start seeing cast iron where it matters, better motors, and enough quality that you're working with the tool instead of fighting it. Some saws here have a small upgrade path too, so a better blade or fence can improve them.

This is a sensible spend for someone committed to woodworking as a hobby but not yet ready to commit to shop-grade equipment. It's also where a lot of buyers find they can do most of what they want without going further.

## $500 to $1,000: the sweet spot for most serious hobbyists

This is where a lot of dedicated woodworkers land, and for good reason. You're getting the 14-inch floor-standing saws that anchor most home shops, with cast iron tables, real fences, and the rigidity that makes cuts clean and accurate. Owners at this level talk about tuning and upgrading their saws rather than replacing them, which tells you these are machines built to keep.

> If you already know you're serious about woodworking and you want one saw you'll grow into rather than out of, this is usually the range worth stretching for. The jump from the tier below to here is the single most meaningful upgrade in cut quality, capacity, and longevity that most buyers will feel. It's the spend that tends to pay for itself in not having to buy twice.

Many saws in this range also accept a riser block, which increases resaw height for far less than the cost of a bigger saw. That's worth knowing if resawing matters to you, because it lets a 14-inch saw punch above its size.

## $1,000 to $2,500: near-daily and production use

Above the sweet spot, you're paying for saws built for heavier, more frequent use. Bigger motors, more resaw height out of the box, heavier construction, and better fences and guides. The people buying here run their saws hard, do production work, or simply want accuracy and capacity beyond what the tier below offers.

For a hobbyist who uses the tool casually, this is more saw than the work calls for. For someone whose shop time is serious and regular, the extra capability earns its keep.

## $2,500 and up: industrial territory

At the top you're into large, heavy saws built for continuous duty, often needing dedicated power. These are for professionals, production shops, and institutions. The build quality and capacity are excellent, but the buyer pool is small and specialized. Unless you're running a shop or doing genuinely demanding work daily, this is more than you need.

## So how much should you spend

Match the spend to the work, and be honest with yourself about how much you'll really use it. If you're testing the waters or cutting light stock occasionally, the entry level keeps your risk low. If you're committed and want a saw you'll keep for years, the $500 to $1,000 range is where most serious hobbyists find the best balance of capability and value, and it's the jump most worth stretching for. Beyond that, you're paying for capacity and duty most hobbyists won't fully use.

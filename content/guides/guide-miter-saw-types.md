Miter saws all do the same basic job, crosscutting boards and cutting angles for trim, framing, and furniture parts. What separates the types is how the head moves: whether it tilts, whether it slides, and how far. Those two things decide what you can actually cut, so the type matters more than any single spec. Here's what each one does and how to figure out which fits your work.

## Standard miter saws

A standard miter saw, sometimes called a chop saw for wood, only swings left and right to cut miter angles. The blade doesn't tilt, so it can't cut a bevel, and the head doesn't slide, so crosscut width is limited to what the blade can reach in one drop. They're cheap and simple, and you rarely see them sold new anymore because compound saws cost little more and do more. If you only ever cut square crosscuts and simple miters in narrow stock, one will do the job, but most buyers skip straight past this type.

## Compound miter saws

A compound miter saw adds a tilting head, so on top of the left-right miter it can lean the blade over to cut a bevel. That combination is what lets you cut the compound angles that crown molding and picture frames need. Compound saws come in two flavors. A single-bevel saw tilts only one direction, so for a matching cut on the other side you flip the workpiece over. A dual-bevel saw tilts both ways, which saves the flipping and speeds up trim work.

Compound saws don't slide, so the crosscut width is capped by the blade. A 10-inch saw crosses roughly a 2x6, and a 12-inch saw handles about a 2x8. For a lot of DIY and trim work that's plenty, and a good non-sliding compound is often more rigid and cheaper than a slider of the same quality.

## Sliding compound miter saws

A sliding compound saw puts the head on rails so it can pull toward you through the cut. That's the feature that lets you crosscut wide boards, usually 12 inches or more, which a non-sliding saw can't reach. If you cut shelving, wide trim, stair treads, or anything past a 2x8, a slider is the type you want.

The tradeoffs are size, weight, and cost. The rails add bulk and often need room behind the saw unless the design routes around it, and a slider has more moving parts that can develop play over time. Cut quality still comes down to the blade and the fence, but the slide is what buys you the width.

## Dual-bevel sliding compound miter saws

This is the do-everything type, and it's where most serious buyers land. It tilts both directions for bevels and it slides for wide crosscuts, so there's very little it can't handle in a trim or furniture shop. Most of the mid-range and premium market is built around this design.

You pay for the capability in price and weight, and the extra mechanisms mean setup and squareness matter more. If you cut a range of stock and don't want the saw to be the limit, this is the class to shop, and it's why most of the saws people cross-shop are dual-bevel sliders.

> If you mostly cut narrow trim and simple angles, a dual-bevel compound saw is compact, rigid, and affordable. If you cut wide boards or a mix of everything, a dual-bevel sliding compound saw is the one to build around. Single-bevel saves a little money if you don't mind flipping stock, and a plain standard saw only makes sense at the very bottom of the budget.

## Blade size: 10-inch vs 12-inch

Most miter saws come with a 10 or 12-inch blade, and it's a real capacity difference. A 12-inch saw cuts taller and wider, which matters for tall baseboard stood against the fence and for crown. A 10-inch blade runs a little faster and the blades cost less, and a 10-inch slider can still cross a wide board because the slide, not the blade, sets the width. If you cut tall material vertically, lean toward 12-inch. If you want lighter weight and cheaper blades and you're buying a slider anyway, 10-inch is fine.

## A note on accuracy and detents

One thing spec sheets don't tell you is how well a saw holds a set angle. Miter saws have positive stops, the detents that click in at common angles like 45 and 22.5 degrees, and the quality of those stops varies a lot from saw to saw. A saw with play in its detents can index to a slightly different angle each time, which shows up as gaps in a miter joint. That's a big deal for furniture and fine trim and less of one for framing. It's the kind of thing owners report clearly even when the maker doesn't publish it, so it's worth weighing alongside the specs.

## So which one is right for you

Start with what you cut. If your work is narrow trim and framing, a dual-bevel compound saw is compact and precise and saves money. If you cut wide stock, shelving, or a bit of everything, a dual-bevel sliding compound saw is the safe long-term pick. Match the blade size to how much tall material you cut, and once you're inside a type, weigh accuracy and detent quality as much as raw capacity.

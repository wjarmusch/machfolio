Band saws get described by a single number, like a 14-inch saw or a 9-inch saw. That number is the first thing people see and one of the more misunderstood specs in the shop. It doesn't tell you how tall a cut you can make, and it doesn't tell you how powerful the saw is. Here's what it truly means & how to use it to pick the right machine.

## What the size number means

The size of a band saw refers to its throat capacity, which is the distance from the blade to the vertical frame of the saw. On a 14-inch saw, that gap is roughly 14 inches. It sets the widest piece of material you can pass to the left of the blade, which matters most when you're cutting curves in a wide panel and need room for the workpiece to swing.

The number doesn't describe cutting height. That is a separate spec called resaw capacity, and it is the one that trips people up.

## Resaw capacity matters more than most people expect

Resaw capacity is how tall a piece you can stand on edge and cut through. It's what lets you take a thick board and slice it into two thinner ones, or cut veneer. Two saws can share the same throat size and have very different resaw heights, so if resawing is something you care about, check that number specifically rather than trusting the size on the label.

A common frustration is buying a saw for resawing and finding the motor or the height comes up short. Smaller benchtop saws often top out around 3 to 4 inches of cut height, which is fine for general curve cutting but limiting for resawing wide stock.

## Benchtop versus floor-standing

This is the real decision most buyers need to make, and it maps closely to how serious you are about the work.

Benchtop saws sit on a table or a stand and tend to be 9 to 10 inches in size. They are compact, they cost less, and they handle scrollwork, small curves, and light cutting well. The tradeoffs are limited resaw height, smaller motors that can bog down in thick hardwood, and less rigidity, which affects cut accuracy. If your space is tight or you are not sure how much you will use it, a benchtop saw is a low-risk way to start.

> If you are buying your first band saw and mostly cutting curves in thinner stock, a quality benchtop saw is a sensible entry point. If you already know you want to resaw or work with thick hardwood regularly, skip the benchtop step and start with a 14-inch floor-standing saw. Buying twice usually costs more than buying the right one once.

Floor-standing saws, most commonly 14-inch models, are the workhorses of the category. They give you more cutting height, more powerful motors, better fences, and the rigidity that makes cuts cleaner. Many of them also accept a riser block, an add-on that increases resaw height significantly for less than the cost of a bigger saw. The 14-inch floor-standing saw is the size most serious hobbyists settle on, which is why so much of the community conversation centers there.

## The 14-inch question

If you spend any time in woodworking forums, you will notice the 14-inch band saw comes up constantly. There is a reason. It hits the sweet spot for a home shop: enough capacity for real work, a price that a committed hobbyist can justify, and a huge ecosystem of blades, fences, and upgrades. If you are trying to buy one machine that will grow with you rather than something you replace in a year, the 14-inch floor-standing saw is often the answer.

## So which one is right for you

Match the saw to the work, not the other way around. If you are cutting small curves, doing craft and scroll work, or just testing whether you enjoy the tool, a benchtop saw keeps your risk and cost low. If you want to resaw, cut thick hardwood, or buy a machine you will keep for years, go straight to a 14-inch floor-standing saw & look for one that accepts a riser block if resaw height matters to you.

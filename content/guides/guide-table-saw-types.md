Table saws come in four broad classes, and the class matters more than any single spec when you're deciding what to buy. A jobsite saw and a cabinet saw might both cut a board to width, but they are built for completely different people and different work. Here's what separates them and how to figure out where you land.

## Jobsite saws

Jobsite saws are the smallest and most portable. They are built to be carried to a work site, folded onto a stand, and run off a standard outlet. Motors are universal type, which means they are loud and higher revving but light. The tabletop is usually aluminum, and the whole saw is designed to be moved often.

They are a strong choice for someone tight on space, working out of a garage that doubles as parking, or doing a mix of construction and woodworking. The tradeoffs are a smaller table, more vibration, and less of the dead-flat precision that finer woodworking rewards. For a lot of beginners and DIY builders, though, a good jobsite saw does everything they need for years.

## Contractor saws

Contractor saws are a step up in size and weight. They came from the era when a contractor wanted something more capable than a jobsite saw but still movable. They have larger cast iron tables, a belt-driven induction motor that runs quieter and smoother, and better fences than most jobsite saws. They usually sit on an open leg stand.

The upside is a meaningful jump in cut quality and comfort over a jobsite saw. The downside is they are heavy and not truly portable, and their dust collection tends to be poor because the motor hangs off the back in an open design. They occupy an interesting middle ground that hybrid saws have largely absorbed, which is worth knowing before you shop.

## Hybrid saws

Hybrid saws blend the contractor and cabinet designs. They take the enclosed cabinet base of a bigger saw, which improves dust collection and stability, and pair it with a smaller motor that runs on a standard household circuit. For a serious hobbyist with a dedicated shop, the hybrid is often the practical sweet spot.

You get cast iron, a good fence, an enclosed base that actually captures dust, and the rigidity that makes cuts clean, without needing the special wiring a full cabinet saw usually requires. If you've outgrown a jobsite or contractor saw and want shop-grade results without rewiring your garage, this is usually the class to look at.

> If you have a dedicated shop space and you are buying a saw to keep for years, a hybrid saw gives you most of what a cabinet saw does at a friendlier price and on standard power. If you run a small business, cut a lot of hardwood, or want a saw that will never be the limiting factor, a cabinet saw is worth the jump. And if you need to move the saw often or space is tight, a quality jobsite saw beats a stationary saw you have no room for.

## Cabinet saws

Cabinet saws are the top of the category. They are heavy, fully enclosed, and built around powerful induction motors that usually need a 220-volt circuit. The mass is the point. All that cast iron soaks up vibration, holds alignment, and delivers the flattest, most accurate cuts you can get from a table saw. Fences and trunnions are heavier duty, and the enclosed cabinet makes dust collection excellent.

They are built for daily use, production work, small shops, and buyers who want accuracy above everything. The tradeoffs are cost, weight, the floor space they claim permanently, and the wiring many of them need. For the buyer who wants a saw that will outlast them and never hold their work back, a cabinet saw is the answer.

## So which one is right for you

Start with your space and your power, because those two can rule options in or out fast. If you have to move the saw or share the space with a car, a jobsite saw is likely your class. If you have a dedicated shop on standard household power, a hybrid saw hits the best balance for most serious hobbyists. If you have 220-volt power, room to spare, and you cut a lot or demand maximum accuracy, a cabinet saw earns its keep. Contractor saws still make sense if you find a good deal on one, though hybrids have largely taken over their spot.
